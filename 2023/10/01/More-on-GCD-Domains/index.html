More on GCD Domains

Posted on 2023-10-01 Edited on 2023-10-02 In Notes on Abstract Algebra

Concepts

For an integral domain $R$, we have defined associates and divisibility, and here is something more about them:

  • associates: invertible elements of $R$ form a field (say $F$), and associates can be described as equivalence class distribution with the equivalence relation $a \sim b \Leftrightarrow \exists x \in F, s.t. ax = b$
    • why it’s an equivalence relation:
      • reflexive: $1\in F$
      • symmetric: $x \in F \Rightarrow x^{-1} \in F$
      • transitive: $ax =b, by = c \Rightarrow axy = c$ and $xy \in F$
    • analogy with number theory: recall that $\mathbb Z$ has all irreversible elements but 1 and in number theory we are essentially studying ‘all irreversible elements in $\mathbb Z$’ plus one unity. No surprise we’ll mainly focus on the irreversible elements when we discuss divisibility for a general integral domain. What’s more, studying divisibility is essentially trying to tell when we can do division — or, undo a multiplication — for elements with no multiplicative inverse.
  • divisibility: it actually gives a partial order by assuming $a < b$ iff $a \mid b$

For $a, b \in R$, we define

  • $d$ to be a common divisor if $d \mid a, d \mid b$
  • $d$ to be a maximal common divisor if $d$ is a common divisor and does not divide any “other” (up to associate) common divisor
  • $d$ to be a greatest common divisor (GCD) if $d$ is a common divisor and can be divided by all common divisors.

Note that in general integral domain

  • GCD may not exist.
    • An example is $R = \mathbb [\sqrt{-3}], a = 4 = 2 \times 2 = (1 + \sqrt 3 ) ( 1 - \sqrt 3 ), b = 2 ( 1 + \sqrt 3)$. By definition one can see that $2, (1 + \sqrt 3)$ are both maximal common divisors and they are incomparable, so there is no “greatest” common divisor.
  • GCD (if exists) is unique up to associate.
  • There might be several different (up to associate) maximal common divisors. However, if GCD exists, GCD would be the only (up to associate) maximal common divisor.

If GCD of any two elements in $R$ exists, we say $R$ is a GCD domain, and we denote GCD of $a, b$ by $(a, b)$.

How To Obtain a UFD?

A Unique Factorization Domain (UFD) is defined to be an integral domain where every non-zero non-unit element can be written as a product of irreducible elements, uniquely up to order and units.

  • You may want to recall that in general integral domain irreducible element is not necessarily prime. An example is $(1 + \sqrt{-3})$ in $\mathbb Z [\sqrt{-3}]$, which is irreducible but not a prime.
  • However, for a UFD, irreducible necessarily implies prime. Because if $a \mid bc$ and $a \nmid b, a \nmid c$, then the number $bc$ would have two different (up to associate and orders) factorizations, namely $at$ (for some $t \in R$) and $bc$, and $R$ is hence not a UFD.

This definition provides little help when we are trying to conclude which rings are UFD’s. To obtain some necessary and sufficient conditions (which might be easier to verify) for this definition, we divide the definition into 2 parts. Namely, we require a UFD to satisfy:

  1. Any element in $R$ can be factorized into product of finite irreducibles
    1. Note that all PID meet this requirement, which can be deprived from the fact that in PID any strictly increasing chain of ideals must be finite in length.
  2. Factorization of any element in $R$ is unique up to associate and orders
    1. An amazing fact is that, the following seemingly weak propositions are all equivalent to this requirement:
      1. irreducible implies prime in $R$
        1. sketch of proof: 1) necessity is already shown above. 2) sufficiency: assume $a$ has two different factorizations $a = p_1 p_2 \cdots p_r = q_1q_2 \cdots q_s$. For every $p_i$, as $p_i$ is prime and $p_i \mid q_1q_2 \cdots q_s( = a)$, we conclude exist some $q_j$ s.t. $q_j = p_i$. Cancel $p_i, q_j$ on each side and continue this process, we can eventually conclude that the two factorizations are identical up to associate and order.
      2. for arbitrary $x, y \in R$, their GCD is unique up to associate
        1. sketch of proof: 1) sufficiency: assume $R$ is not UFD, then $\exists q, x, y, a, b \in R, q = xy = ab$ and $x \not\sim a, x\not \sim b$, then $x$ and $a$ are different (up to associate) maximal common divisors of $ax$ and $q$, which reaches a contradiction. 2) necessity: for any $x, y \in R$, let their unique factorization be $x = p_1 \cdots p_r, y = p’_1 \cdots p’_s$ and let $q_1, q_2, \cdots, q_n$ include all different (up to associate) prime divisors of $x$ and $y$. Then both $x, y$ can be written uniquely in the form $u \prod q_i ^{e_i}$(where $u$ is a unit), and one can verify that $(x, y) = \prod q_i^{\min(e_i, e’_i)}$.
        2. We can conclude that UFD is always GCD domain. However, the inverse is not true, as the ascending chain condition on principal ideals may not hold.

Bézout’s Lemma?

In number theory, Bézout’s lemma (which says $\forall a, b \in \mathbb Z, \exists x, y\in \mathbb Z, s.t. ax + by = (a, b)$ ) gives a very useful property for GCD. Sadly this is not true for UFD in general. A counter-example is given as follows: in $\mathbb Z[x]$, $(x, 2) = 1$, but for arbitrary $f, g \in \mathbb Z[x]$ we know $f \cdot x + g \cdot 2 \in \langle 2, x \rangle$, and this ideal does not contain 1. However, one can verify that this property is true for PID, as $(a, b)$ is exactly generator of the ideal $\langle a, b \rangle$.
